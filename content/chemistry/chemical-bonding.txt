Chemical bonding is the electrostatic attraction between atoms, ions or molecules that enables the formation of chemical compounds. A bond may result from the electrostatic force of attraction between oppositely charged ions as in ionic bonds or through the sharing of electrons as in covalent bonds. The strength of chemical bonds varies considerably; there are "strong bonds" or "primary bonds" such as covalent, ionic and metallic bonds, and "weak bonds" or "secondary bonds" such as dipole-dipole interactions, the London dispersion force and hydrogen bonding. In ionic bonding, valence electrons are transferred from one atom to another (usually from metals to non-metals), creating positive cations and negative anions. The electrostatic attraction between these oppositely charged ions binds them together into crystal lattices. Covalent bonding involves the stable sharing of electron pairs between atoms, typically non-metals, to achieve stable valence configurations. Hybridization is the concept of mixing atomic orbitals into new hybrid orbitals suitable for electron pairing properties.
